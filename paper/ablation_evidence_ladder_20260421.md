## Read

- Promote hub dictionaries, sticky/feature routing, mixed-bit frontiers, and verifier stop rules only as an interaction stack with matched controls.
- The route-conditioned hub sweep shows that the current frontier and stop heuristics are not drop-in additive components: the best frontier gain is only `+0.0104`, oracle frontier is negative, and the stop rule never adds positive accuracy.
- The route-class patch follow-up shows that calibration-aware local protection only ties the current quant-error frontier and route-class frontier pruning is still negative, so the next fix should move up to the hub/interface or pruning-rule level.
- The multi-way canonical-hub follow-up adds a boundary clue rather than a full fix: at `1` shot/class the GPA-style shared basis helps on MSE, but direct family fitting retakes the lead once `2+` paired shots/class are available.
- The gauge-fix quotient follow-up sharpens the symmetry story: once head matching is scored after Procrustes alignment, quotient-aware matching becomes the best non-oracle method at `1` shot/class (`0.0796` MSE), beats direct few-shot fitting (`0.0985`), and recovers the true head correspondence exactly, but direct family fitting still retakes the lead once `2+` paired shots/class are available.
- The composed quotient+GPA+sparse-dictionary follow-up is the strongest low-shot shared-basis result so far: it is best non-oracle at both `1` and `2` shots/class (`0.0568`, `0.0576`), preserves exact head recovery, and only yields back to direct family fitting by `4` shots/class.
- The strong interface-stress follow-up narrows the interface story: under explicit tokenizer-like corruption, the byte/span-remap version becomes the best shared-basis variant at `1` and `2` shots/class (`0.0566`, `0.0570`) and sits close to the oracle-interface ceiling, but it does not change the broader boundary that direct few-shot fitting retakes the lead once paired data is plentiful.
- The byte-sidecar follow-up sharpens that story: under the same strong interface corruption, a tokenizer-agnostic byte sidecar on top of the shared-basis lane lowers MSE much further (`0.0392`, `0.0394` at `1-2` shots/class), outperforming both remap-only and oracle-interface latent-only branches, while still yielding to direct few-shot + remap once paired data is plentiful.
- The sequence-aligned sidecar follow-up sharpens it again: adding sequence-aware interface features on top of the byte sidecar lowers MSE further (`0.0360`, `0.0362` at `1-2` shots/class) and stays the best shared-basis variant at `4-8` shots/class, but direct held-out-family fitting plus remap still wins overall once paired data is abundant.
- The frozen same-pair GSM8K smoke now gives the first real benchmark contract readout for this stage: `C2C` clears the gate (`0.1250` vs target `0.0625`), `text_to_text` regresses (`0.0312`), and the current `rotalign_kv` row only ties target while failing numeric extraction coverage (`28/32`). Treat the contract as valid and the current sparse transport row as a blocker, not as a promotable benchmark result.
- The frozen checkpoint sweep sharpens the real-lane story: `dynalign_module_replace` and `spanalign_module_replace` are the only existing real proxies that improve over target on the same exact contract (`0.0938`), but both still trail `C2C` and only gain on one example; `bytespan_module_replace` regresses and `sae_adapter` collapses. That means the next real method should build on output-aware alignment, not byte-only alignment or the first sparse-codebook proxy.
- The GPA sparse-dictionary follow-up sharpens that low-shot clue: at `1` shot/class the shared sparse dictionary is now the best non-oracle method on MSE (`0.1171` vs `0.1825` for direct few-shot and `0.2355` for canonical-only), but direct family fitting still retakes the lead once `2+` paired shots/class are available, and the current repair gate remains a pure no-op.
- Treat confidence-only routing and fixed-depth repair as blockers, not baselines to keep rerunning.
- Any real-route-pool promotion should carry the same telemetry columns: atom recovery, route stability, bit histogram, stop reason, help/harm, bytes, and latency.
